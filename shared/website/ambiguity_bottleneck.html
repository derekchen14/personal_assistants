§ 01

Where every agent quietly fails

Why care

Math is checkable. Code either compiles or it doesn't. Benchmarks tell you when you're winning. That's why agents look so impressive on coding tasks — and why they stop looking impressive the moment a real user gets involved.

Real users say "make it pop", "clean this up", "end of day". Those phrases mean different things at different companies, in different rooms, in different weeks. There's no verifiable reward to optimize against. There's just whether the person on the other end feels understood.

The honest gap between an agent that benchmarks well and an agent you'd actually deploy is filled almost entirely with ambiguity. And almost no agent SDK treats that as a first-order problem.

§ 02

Three flavors of unclear

The taxonomy

Ambiguity isn't one thing. Treating it as one thing is why every agent handles it badly. Three distinct flavors — each requires a different response.

A

Contextual

The water you swim in. "End of day" is 5pm at a law firm and midnight at a startup. The unwritten rules of your team aren't in training data. They're in heads.

Examples
"the usual tone"
"keep it short"
"don't be too aggressive"
B

Temporal

Ground truth shifts as the agent acts. The post you publish changes which "marathon post" means tomorrow. Yesterday's right answer is today's mistake.

Examples
"latest version"
"the current schedule"
"that one I started yesterday"
C

Communication

Natural language is lossy compression. Users don't know which details matter. "Rework the marathon post" contains an ambiguous noun, an ambiguous verb, and a missing slot.

Examples
"rework the marathon post"
"polish that"
"make it pop"

A typical agent collapses all three into the same response: a confident guess. We collapse them into three different responses: a clarifying question, a re-grounding check, or a confirmation — whichever fits the actual flavor.

§ 03

Our answer

What we built

Ambiguity isn't a failure mode — it's a sensor reading. Every turn, the agent rates how certain it is about each part of the request, and that rating decides whether to ask, confirm, or proceed.

That single design choice cascades. It forces an explicit representation of what we believe right now. It forces real memory across turns and sessions, because you can only ask intelligent questions if you remember the answers. It forces a model of your domain, because confidence about "the marathon post" requires knowing what posts exist.

The architecture follows from the goal: recognize unsure, then act on it. Everything else is in service of that.

In one sentence

"Our assistants run a small understanding module that tracks the degree of ambiguity in a dedicated dialogue state, so the agent always knows when it should stop and ask."

The understanding module is the part most other SDKs skip. We think it's the part that matters most.

Anti-pattern we refuse

Stuffing the entire transcript back into the prompt every turn, hoping the model remembers. That's not memory. That's a recap, performed by the LLM, every single time.
