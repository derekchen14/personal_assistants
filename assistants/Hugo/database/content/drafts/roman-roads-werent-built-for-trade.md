## The Trade Assumption
People picture Roman roads as ancient highways full of merchants hauling wine and grain across the empire. That image gets the purpose backwards. The roads were built by the state for the state, and commerce was a side effect, not the goal.

## Roads as Military Arteries
Rome built roads so legions could march fast and reach a frontier or a revolt before it spread. A straight paved route meant an army could cover a stunning distance in a day and arrive able to fight. The whole network was a tool for projecting force and moving troops, messengers, and supplies for the military.

## How They Were Built
The engineering reflects the mission, with deep layered foundations, drainage, and lines that run brutally straight over hills instead of bending around them for an easier grade. Merchants would have preferred gentler slopes, but soldiers wanted the shortest path, so the shortest path is what got paved. These roads were designed to survive heavy boots and marching columns, not comfortable ox carts.

## The Trade Came Later
Once the roads existed, of course traders used them, and towns and markets grew up along the routes. But that traffic rode on top of infrastructure built for a completely different reason. The trade was the empire making use of its military spine, not the thing that justified building it.
