## The Pass Sounds Like a Deal
A single flat rate for unlimited trains across a whole continent sounds like the obvious win. But the price is set for the traveler who rides constantly, and almost nobody actually rides that much. You end up paying for flexibility you never use.

## When Point Tickets Win
If you know your route, individual point-to-point tickets beat the pass almost every time. Buy each leg separately and you pay for the trains you actually take, nothing more. This is the normal case for most trips, not the exception.

## Booking Windows
The real savings come from booking early. Most rail operators release cheap advance fares two to three months out, and those seats sell first. Wait until the week of travel and you pay double or triple, which is the only situation where the pass starts to look reasonable.

## A Worked Example
Take Paris to Amsterdam to Berlin over ten days. A multi-country pass runs a few hundred dollars, but three advance point tickets come in well under half that. Book them the day fares open and the pass never had a chance.
